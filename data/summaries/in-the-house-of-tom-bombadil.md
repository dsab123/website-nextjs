This was an entertaining read, the most entertaining so far.

If you want to read the book for yourself, I recommend not reading the rest of these thoughts, as I will surely spoil it for you.

Who is Tom Bombadil? If you don't remember, that's okay - Wiley points you to a few chapters in _The Fellowship of the Ring_ to jog your memory. Peter Jackson didn't put Bombadil in the movie adaptations, but he was in _Rings of Power_. We unfortunately did not find the latter enjoyable enough to keep watching (I was game, I blame my wife), and this book came out before the show aired, so I don't know how Wiley, to say nothing of Tolkien, would have received Bombadil as portrayed.

Anyway, Bombadil seems to come out of nowhere and rescue the hobbits from sure destruction at the hands of an evil tree's digestive tract. He saves them again shortly thereafter. (First at the tree, then at a tomb). He is a mythical figure, wearing primary colors and singing seemingly nonsensensical songs. He just does not _fit_ with the rest of Middle Earth. Tolkien himself said so, and equivocated on whether he was important, essentially saying "he is important, but not to the narrative at hand.":

_Tom Bombadil is not an important person – to the narrative. I suppose he has some importance as a 'comment'. I mean, I do not really write like that: he is just an invention, and he represents something that I feel important, though I would not be prepared to analyze the feeling precisely. I would not, however, have left him in, if he did not have some kind of function._

Weaving together communications from Tolkien to others, as well as from his other writings, C.R. Wiley takes up the task of learning more about the identity of this strange character. Tom Bombadil seems to live in Eden, unbothered by the Barrow-Downs (a field of tombs) near him, spending his days as "Master", taking care of the animals and the land in a way that lets them flourish. Bombadil exercises godly dominion (not domination) over his wife Goldberry; though she lives with him now, she is still a River-Woman's daughter, so he brings her water-lillies and enthrones her around them. You get the sense that he would not have laid the frame of his house with 2x4s, but rather from whole trunks of trees long dead.

Far from being delusional, however, he is clearly aware of the evils surrounding his little happy plot of land. And he knows of the Dark Lord. When he _commands_ Frodo to give him the ring, he toys with it - puts it up to his eye like a monocle, reminding the young hobbits of the Eye of Sauron; pushes it down onto his little finger, but does not disappear; and performs an old-man magic trick to make it disappear, before giving it back to Frodo with a big smile. This is a man who is "out of this world". The temptations of power seem to have no hold on him.

  Wiley makes a solid case that Bombadil represents the _End_. I take _End_ here to mean the end of all things, when according to the Scriptures peace is restored and evil is banished. Then, as Micah 4:4 says, "they shall sit every man under his vine and under his fig tree". The man who lives solely in the age to come does not worry himself with affairs of the present age. Christians live in the overlap of the ages, so it is ours, at least in part, to do the work in front of us. Bombadil could not have taken the Ring to Mordor, it was not his calling. Just so, you and I have our respective callings from God.
