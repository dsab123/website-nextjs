I have the privilege of serving as an elder at [Acacia Reformed Church](https://arcopc.org).

Our practice during morning worship is to read through a chapter of the bible in addition to the passage that is preached, and to briefly summarize it and show how it teaches us about Christ. We're making our way through Isaiah right now.

Here are my biblical-theological reflections on Isaiah 36 through 45.

You can read the earlier posts in this series here:
- [Isaiah 25-35](/blog/49/isaiah-25-35-biblical-theology-readings)

### Isaiah 36

The day the Southern Kingdom has feared has arrived. The King of Assyria, against whom Judah has formed an alliance with even Egypt, their former slave owners, has begun to annex their land.

This Rabshakeh, deputy of Sennacherib, speaks truly of the folly of trusting in Egypt. But with silver-tongued speech he tries to lure God's people into a false hope for the future. His promise sounds eerily similar to God's promise of a permanent heavenly home.

Why do the people not take the Rabshakeh at his word and defect?

"If the King can trust in the Lord, then so can I", the hearers must have thought.

We can apply this to ourselves and our King of Kings: if God's promises were sure enough for Jesus to depend on, then surely they are for me too. In Him all God's promises are Yes and Amen.


### Isaiah 37

Hezekiah demonstrates the behavior proper to a king who trusts in God, and with his vindication a zeal for God's glory, see v 20.

God's responses through Isaiah reveal several treasures for us as God's people:

In verse 23 - God considers the mocking and reviling of His people to be against Him. We are the apple of His eye because of our ingrafting into His covenant community through the Lord Jesus!

In verse 26 - God's promise that he knows us intimately, our sitting down and our going out and coming in, is a comfort to those who love and worship Him. To the wicked it is sheer terror.

And how does the Lord deliver Hezekiah and the people? By a supernatural deliverance.

Isaiah prophesied in chapter 31 that "the Assyrian shall fall by a sword, not of man." An angel encamps around them at night and struck down almost 200,000 of Sennacherib's forces! And later takes his life by his own children.

How does this point us to the Lord Jesus? Jesus is the greater king who submitted Himself to God's providence. He was not spared the suffering of death, but was indeed delivered because of His godly fear. And he has won for His people not just a temporal victory, but an eternal one!


### Isaiah 38

Hezekiah goes from a great supernatural victory over Assyria to a deep low. The Lord, who won the great victory for the king, now informs him that he is going to die, and to set his house in order. Like in the Lord is like that. He teaches us to learn how to abound as well as to be broght low.

Hezekiah was a man of prayer, if anything else. He takes his petition to the Lord. The Lord immediately hears and promises to extend his life.

Following the Lord's salvation, he sings a song drawn from the Psalms as well as Job's prayers. This is a common pattern among the biblical characters - Mary, Hannah, Jonah to name a few.

"The LORD will save me, and we will play my music on stringed instruments all the days of our lives, at the house of the LORD."

A king leading his people in song at the house of the Lord forever.

Listen to Hebrews 2:12, which speaks of the Lord Jesus upon His resurrection and ascension: "I will tell of Your Name to my brothers; in the midst of the congregation I will sing Your praise."

Jesus, after his deliverance, was raised up from the dead and leads His people in song to the Lord. He sings over us!


### Isaiah 40

Isaiah in divided into two books, the first one ends at Chapter 39 which we read last week.

We saw the beginning of the tragic end of Hezekiah, who did not mind the coming disaster upon his people due his sin, since it would be after his days. We're left longing for stability for Israel.

We begin Book II with this chapter. "Comfort, comfort", the Lord promises rescue and deliverance for His people, battered and bruised because of their own sin.

He reminds them who He is, the Creator of all life, to whom the nations, are like a drop in the bucket. To compare the gods of these nations to Him is incredulous.

This is good news, this is the Gospel for His people, because He has bound Himself to them. Though they grow weary and faint, and we do too, He has endless power in wait for those who wait upon Him.

But He does not just supply power from afar. In His union with them, God sent a forerunner to prepare His people to receive Him. We know that forerunner as John, and we know our God as Jesus.


### Isaiah 41

By way of reminder we're in Book I of Isaiah, which begins in chapter 40. This book is known for its overflowing comfort to God's people.

Here God challenges the coastlands, among whom include Tyre and Sidon, and their gods. Which one can predict the future, calling future events before they can happen?

At this challenge, the coastlands quake with fear.

This is good news for God's people, because God holds them by their right hand, promising them He is with them. Three times He says "fear not!"

He even promises that His people will be part of that great judgement that reduces the wicked from a tough dense mountain to chaff that the wind drives away, reminiscent of Psalm 1.

God's permanent presence with His people is realized fully in Christ, who dwells in His church by His Spirit.


### Isaiah 42

You could title this chapter "The Tale of Two Servants."

What is otherwise confusing in this chapter becomes a beautiful picture of Christ taking our place.

To recap where Israel is at this point in redemptive history - they are failures. They are God's people, chosen by Him to be holy and to propagate the knowledge of Him to the world. They are to be an example to the world of what a loving communion with God looks like.

If we look at the second half of the chapter, we see Israel as this servant - blind, wretched, deaf, without ears to hear. So the Lord gives him up to severe chastening.

At the beginning of the chapter, though, the Lord delights in His chosen servant, His elect one. He says to us, "Behold this One!" He is merciful. He will not put out the faintly burning wick, which gives more smoke than light, He is a tender shepherd. He does not give up, but extends God's rule all the way to the rebellious coastlands from chapter 41.

The Lord speaks to this beloved servant words of encouragement.

That beloved servant is the Lord Jesus, the better Israel. He comes in place of us to live the righteous life we should live. And we can respond like the coastlands who have been brought into His kingdom, with singing!


### Isaiah 43

God's relationship with Israel at this point is messy. And not because of God. This is the only broken relationship where one side is completely at fault.

The Lord has promised His presence with them when they enter into all manner of affliction. He promises to plunder the nations in His effort to reunite them. He even plans to use them as His witnesses in His campaign to destroy all foreign gods and restore the world's equilibrium.

Israel's response we see in verse 22 and following. I have not burdened you, but you have burdened Me with your sins, the Lord says.

Because He is faithful, He does not go back on His Word. He promises to blot out their transgressions.

He also promises suffering for His Servant Israel. Even from the days of Adam, their first father, and Moses, their mediator, the Lord remembers their sin. It must be put away for right relationship.

Our Savior, the suffering servant, the second Adam, the true mediator, has accomplished this restoration. Amen!


### Isaiah 44

The last chapter left Israel facing affliction and chastening from the Lord because of their sin. But God is faithful to His covenant promises and will not forsake them.

There is more of the same in this chapter.

Amid the idolatry of the nations, and their own chastening by the Lord’s hand, God wants His people to remember that He is their God.

And He promises to slake their thirst with His Spirit. They have become dry and arid because of their sin, but the Lord will restore them with living waters, creating a garden of Eden in its place.

He even names Cyrus, not to be born for hundreds of years, to do this.

Christ did that. He gives the Spirit without measure, that we might know and receive the things freely given to us by Him.

And our response is to break forth into singing with all creation.


### Isaiah 45

God has Isaiah has been challenging other gods to predict the future before it happens. Of course, God knows the future because He has declared the end from the beginning.

Here he puts His foreknowledge on display, for His glory and His people's good.

He speaks to Cyrus more than 100 years before his birth. He calls him "His anointed", and equips him and makes his way level that he would rebuild Jerusalem, he says in verse 13.

This is in service to God's ultimate Goal, to unite peoples from all over the earth under His allegiance: "Turn to me and be saved, all the ends of the earth!" in verse 22.

God's dealings with Cyrus is a picture of His dealings with our Savior, who did greater works than he. Cyrus was not born of a virgin, nor did he suffer bodily for His people. But our Savior did. And to Jesus, not Cyrus, will every knee bow at the last day!
